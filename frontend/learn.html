Cyber Safety Learning Center

Equip yourself with the knowledge to identify and prevent online scams, harassment, and social engineering attacks.

Romance scams occur when a criminal adopts a fake online identity to gain a victim's affection and trust. The scammer then uses the illusion of a romantic or close relationship to manipulate and/or steal from the victim.

Common Red Flags:

  • They express strong emotions or declare love very quickly.
  • They quickly ask to move the conversation off the dating app to WhatsApp or email.
  • They claim to live, work, or travel outside the country (e.g., military, oil rig worker, international doctor).
  • They make repeated excuses about why they cannot meet in person or join a video call.
  • They eventually ask for money for an "emergency", travel expenses, or a lucrative "investment."
Safety Rule: Never send money, crypto, or gift cards to someone you have only communicated with online or by phone.

Scammers target job seekers with fake employment opportunities to steal personal information or money. These scams often promise high pay for easy work with flexible hours.

Common Red Flags:

  • You receive a job offer on WhatsApp or Telegram from an unknown "recruiter".
  • The pay is unusually high for simple tasks (like liking YouTube videos or leaving reviews).
  • You are asked to pay an "onboarding fee", buy training software, or purchase equipment from a specific vendor using a fake check they provide.
  • The communication is unprofessional and filled with grammatical errors.
Safety Rule: Legitimate employers will never ask you to pay them to secure a job or demand your banking passwords for direct deposit setup.

Sextortion is a serious crime where someone threatens to distribute private or sensitive material (often photos or videos) if you don't pay them or send them more content.

What to do if you are targeted:

  • Do not pay the ransom. Scammers rarely delete the material even if paid; they often just demand more money.
  • Stop communication immediately. Block the perpetrator across all platforms.
  • Lock down your accounts. Set all your social media profiles to the highest privacy settings.
  • Collect evidence. Take screenshots of the threats, profile names, and conversation history before blocking.
  • Report it. Report the account to the platform and contact local authorities or organizations like the Cyber Civil Rights Initiative.

Phishing is a deceptive practice where attackers send fraudulent messages designed to trick you into revealing sensitive information or deploying malicious software on your infrastructure.

How to Spot Fake Links:

  • Look for Typo-squatting: Scammers use domains that look similar to real ones (e.g., amzon.com instead of amazon.com).
  • Check the Sender: Do not just look at the display name. Tap the name to see the actual email address it was sent from.
  • Don't Trust the Padlock: Just because a website has HTTPS (the padlock icon) does not mean it is safe—it just means the connection to the scam site is encrypted.
  • Beware of Urgency: Phishing emails often threaten account suspension or promise a reward if you act "immediately".

CyberGuard's AI analyzes links to give you a Trust Score (0-100). A score below 40 means the site is highly likely to be malicious. To understand how we calculate this, it helps to understand the anatomy of a URL.

The Anatomy of a URL:

https://www.amazon.com/products/kindle
  • Protocol (https://): Tells the browser how to connect. 's' stands for secure (encrypted). CyberGuard deducts points if a site only uses 'http'.
  • Domain Name (amazon): The actual name of the website. CyberGuard AI scans this for misleading hyphens (e.g., ama-zon) or suspicious keywords like "login", "free-gift", or "verify".
  • Top-Level Domain (.com): The extension. Scammers often use cheap or free extensions. CyberGuard flags unusual TLDs like .xyz, .top, or .ml as high-risk.
  • Path (/products/...): Points to a specific page on that server. Scammers sometimes hide the real domain in the path to trick you!

How CyberGuard Calculates Your Trust Score:

Every link starts with a perfect 100 Trust Score. Our AI then deducts points for suspicious patterns:

  • -40 points if the link uses a raw IP address (like 192.168.1.1) instead of a domain name.
  • -15 points (each) for manipulative keywords in the domain like "verify", "secure", or "prize".
  • -20 points for excessive hyphens designed to look like a complex subdomain.
  • -30 points for using cheap, spam-associated Top-Level Domains.
  • -10 points if the URL is excessively long, often used to hide the true destination.
